# Found in Noise
 
*An Autogenous Multichannel Fixed-Media Composition*
 
Final Project Documentation of Spatial Audio II

 
## Inspiration

The primary inspiration for our project comes from Alvin Lucier’s work *I Am Sitting in a Room* (1969).
In Lucier’s creative process, he sat in a room holding a tape recorder and a microphone, recording himself reading a short text. The text begins: "I am sitting in a room, different from the one you are in now. I am recording the sound of my speaking voice..." He then played this recording back into the same room through a loudspeaker and used the microphone to record it onto a second tape. Next, he played this second tape back into the room and recorded a third tape. He repeated this process 32 times. Throughout the entire playback of this piece, what you hear is a slow evolution. In the initial generations of recordings, his voice is clearly discernible—you can make out every word. However, with each cycle of playback within the room, the room's inherent resonant frequencies (the pitches that the room naturally amplifies) become slightly stronger, while those frequencies absorbed by the room become slightly weaker. By around the 10th generation, the words begin to blur. By the 20th generation, the words have vanished entirely—the consonantal elements have completely dissolved, leaving behind only a humming stream of sound filled with specific pitches. In the final generation of recordings, no trace of speech remains; all that is left is the room itself.

Lucier did not intend to create a work merely about the gradual deterioration of the human voice, nor did he aim simply to demonstrate a clever recording technique. The conceptual intent behind the work is far more profound—and it is precisely this aspect that establishes it as a foundational work in the field of "process-based sound art." The room is the true composer. Every room possesses its own unique acoustic characteristics—specifically, its distinct resonant frequencies—which are collectively determined by the room's dimensions, materials, and shape. Typically, we are unable to directly perceive this unique acoustic signature, as it is invariably masked by the various sound sources active within the room. The creative process employed by Lucier is designed precisely to enable the room to "hear" its own voice. Through this recursive, cyclical operation, the human voice—acting as the initial "sound source"—is progressively stripped away and filtered out, until ultimately, nothing remains but the pure "sound" of the room itself.

Our project extends this idea beyond a single-channel or mono context into a spatial audio environment. Rather than treating space as a passive container, we approach it as an active system that reshapes sound through both physical acoustics and spatial distribution. This shift allows us to explore how multi-channel playback influences the iterative transformation process that Lucier originally demonstrated.

## Concept

The core concept of this project is the combination of temporal iteration and spatial transformation. Like Lucier's original process, we employ repeated cycles of playback and recording. However, instead of maintaining a fixed channel format, each iteration introduces spatialization across an eight-speaker system. In this framework, sound is not only filtered by the room but also reconfigured in space during each iteration. The system effectively becomes a feedback loop in which spatial encoding, acoustic propagation, and recording continuously interact. As a result, the final output reflects not only the resonant characteristics of the room but also the structural influence of the speaker arrangement and spatial processing.

The project is implemented in PureData, with the Zerr~ externals handling the spatialization. The key idea behind Zerr is that the spatial behavior of a sound is derived from the sound itself rather than imposed on it. In our patch, the input audio is continuously analyzed for its spectral centroid — a descriptor that tracks the "center of mass" of the sound's frequency content over time. This single feature drives the entire spatial response: the centroid value controls a set of eight gain envelopes, one per speaker, which together determine how the sound is dispersed across the array. Bright, spectrally unstable input activates multiple envelopes at once and spreads the sound across many speakers; stable input produces sparse envelope activity and focuses the sound to fewer points. No manual panning is involved.
We also customized two parameters that shape the character of the spatial movement. A custom trajectory ordering (1 8 2 7 3 6 4 5) pairs opposite speakers rather than walking them sequentially, so the sound leaps across the room instead of orbiting the listener. A topological "neighbor graph" defines which speakers are considered logically adjacent — independent of their physical positions — and the spatial trajectory walks this graph, producing non-circular, almost teleporting motion.

## Technology

The project is implemented using PureData as the central environment for audio routing and processing. Within Pd, we used the Zerr plugin to transform stereo input signals into an eight-channel spatial output. The Zerr plugin plays a critical role in distributing audio across the speaker array, enabling spatial diffusion and positioning. Combined with Pd’s flexible signal flow design, this allows us to construct a recursive system in which each recorded iteration is reintroduced into the spatialization process. The hardware setup consists of an eight-speaker configuration and a microphone used to capture the resulting sound field. The interaction between digital processing and physical acoustics is essential to the behavior of the system.

## System Setup

The speaker system is arranged in an eight-channel configuration, typically in a circular formation surrounding the listening and recording position. A microphone is placed within the space to capture the combined acoustic output of all speakers. The signal flow begins with a stereo input, which is processed in Pure Data and spatialized using the Zerr plugin. The resulting eight-channel signal is played through the speaker system, propagates through the physical space, and is then recorded via microphone. This recorded signal is re-imported into the system and used as the input for the next iteration. Through this setup, each cycle introduces additional layers of spatial and acoustic transformation, gradually reshaping the original material.

## Implementation

The implementation follows a recursive workflow in which a stereo source is first introduced into the system and spatialized across eight channels. This signal is then played back through the speaker array and recorded in the room. The recorded audio is subsequently fed back into Pure Data, where it undergoes the same spatialization process before being played again. This cycle is repeated six times. With each iteration, the accumulated effects of room acoustics, speaker placement, and spatial encoding become more pronounced. The system effectively behaves as a feedback network in which both digital and physical processes contribute to the transformation of sound. An important aspect of the implementation is the interaction between multiple propagation paths. Each speaker emits sound that reaches the microphone through slightly different trajectories, introducing variations in timing, phase, and frequency response. These differences compound over successive iterations, leading to increasingly complex sonic results.

## Results and Observations

An additional factor contributing to the dominance of low frequencies in our final output lies in the spectral characteristics of the source material itself. Unlike Lucier’s use of spoken voice, which contains a relatively balanced distribution of frequencies and relies heavily on mid- and high-frequency components for intelligibility, our initial input consisted primarily of drums, bass, and a limited amount of lead synthesizer. This configuration inherently emphasizes low-frequency energy, particularly through sustained or repetitive bass elements.

Within an iterative playback–recording system, such spectral imbalance becomes increasingly significant. Frequencies with greater initial energy and temporal stability are more likely to persist and be reinforced across successive generations. In contrast, higher-frequency components—often associated with transient details such as percussive attacks or timbral nuances—are more susceptible to attenuation due to room absorption, air damping, microphone response, and phase interference in the multi-speaker setup. As a result, each iteration effectively amplifies the relative dominance of low-frequency content while progressively eliminating higher-frequency detail.

This suggests that, in our system, the long-term spectral outcome is not determined solely by the acoustic properties of the room, but also by the initial distribution of energy in the source material. The iterative process therefore acts not only as a probe of spatial resonance, but also as a mechanism that reveals and exaggerates inherent spectral biases in the input signal.

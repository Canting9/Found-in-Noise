# Found in Noise
 
*An Autogenous Multichannel Fixed-Media Composition*
 
Final Project Documentation of Spatial Audio II

 
## Inspiration

The primary inspiration for our project comes from Alvin Lucier’s work *I Am Sitting in a Room* (1969).
In Lucier’s creative process, he sat in a room holding a tape recorder and a microphone, recording himself reading a short text. The text begins: "I am sitting in a room, different from the one you are in now. I am recording the sound of my speaking voice..." He then played this recording back into the same room through a loudspeaker and used the microphone to record it onto a second tape. Next, he played this second tape back into the room and recorded a third tape. He repeated this process 32 times. Throughout the entire playback of this piece, what you hear is a slow evolution. In the initial generations of recordings, his voice is clearly discernible—you can make out every word. However, with each cycle of playback within the room, the room's inherent resonant frequencies (the pitches that the room naturally amplifies) become slightly stronger, while those frequencies absorbed by the room become slightly weaker. By around the 10th generation, the words begin to blur. By the 20th generation, the words have vanished entirely—the consonantal elements have completely dissolved, leaving behind only a humming stream of sound filled with specific pitches. In the final generation of recordings, no trace of speech remains; all that is left is the room itself.

Lucier did not intend to create a work merely about the gradual deterioration of the human voice, nor did he aim simply to demonstrate a clever recording technique. The conceptual intent behind the work is far more profound—and it is precisely this aspect that establishes it as a foundational work in the field of "process-based sound art." The room is the true composer. Every room possesses its own unique acoustic characteristics—specifically, its distinct resonant frequencies—which are collectively determined by the room's dimensions, materials, and shape. Typically, we are unable to directly perceive this unique acoustic signature, as it is invariably masked by the various sound sources active within the room. The creative process employed by Lucier is designed precisely to enable the room to "hear" its own voice. Through this recursive, cyclical operation, the human voice—acting as the initial "sound source"—is progressively stripped away and filtered out, until ultimately, nothing remains but the pure "sound" of the room itself.
